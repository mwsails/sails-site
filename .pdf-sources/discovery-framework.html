The PrinciplePage 2 of 7

Discovery in velocity sales is 20 minutes. Not 45.

Enterprise discovery is an excavation: an hour of layered questions to map a committee's politics. Velocity discovery is a filter: five questions that tell you whether this deal deserves a demo, asked fast enough that the buyer doesn't lose patience.

What velocity discovery has to accomplish

  • Qualify in or out. Does this buyer match the ICP? If not, end gracefully and save both of you an hour.
  • Surface the trigger. Why are they talking to you this week instead of last quarter? The trigger predicts urgency better than anything they say about budget.
  • Get the pain in their words. You'll replay their exact phrasing in the demo. Their words, not your pitch.
  • Map the path to yes. Who signs, what they're comparing you against, and when they want this solved.
  • Book the demo before hanging up. Discovery that ends with "I'll send some times" loses a third of its conversions. Calendar invite goes out during the call.

What it does NOT have to accomplish

  • Building rapport for 10 minutes. Two minutes of human warmth, then work.
  • A full needs analysis. That's what the demo customization is for.
  • Multi-threading. At $2K-$24K ACVs there is usually one buyer. Find them, talk to them.
  • Pricing negotiation. Price comes up at the demo, on your terms, not as a discovery ambush.

The Five QuestionsPage 3 of 7

The five questions, in order.

Order matters. Each question earns the right to ask the next one.

1

The trigger question

"What made you reach out now?" (inbound) or "What made you take this call?" (outbound). The answer reveals urgency, the event that created the search, and often the internal politics. A buyer with a real trigger ("our spreadsheet broke during board prep") closes 3x faster than one who is "just looking around."

2

The current-state question

"How are you handling this today?" Gets you the incumbent — a competitor, a spreadsheet, a manual process, or nothing. Each one changes your demo. Competing with a spreadsheet means selling time savings. Competing with a rival product means selling the delta.

3

The cost question

"What's it costing you to keep doing it that way?" Hours, dollars, deals lost, churn risk, founder sanity. If the buyer can't articulate a cost, the deal will stall at the proposal stage, because nobody buys a fix for a free problem. Help them put a number on it now and the close gets easier later.

If the buyer can't name a cost

"Let me ask it differently: if this stayed exactly the way it is for the next 12 months, what happens?" Silence means low urgency. A wince means there's a cost they haven't said out loud yet.

4

The path question

"If the demo shows you what you need, what happens next on your end?" One question that surfaces three things: who signs, what the approval process looks like, and whether there's a hidden stakeholder. In velocity sales the answer is usually simple ("I'd sign up") — and when it isn't, you just learned that before the demo instead of after the proposal.

5

The timeline question

"When do you want this solved by?" Not "what's your timeline" (invites vagueness) but "when do you want this solved by" (invites a date). Anchor the rest of the deal to their date. If they say "no rush," pair it with the trigger answer: a real trigger with no rush usually means a hidden blocker. Ask about it.

Then stop asking questions

Five questions, honestly answered, is enough to qualify a velocity deal. The instinct to keep digging comes from enterprise training. Resist it. Every additional question past the point of qualification spends buyer patience you'll want at the demo.

The close of the discovery call

Talk track — book the demo live

"Based on what you've told me, this is worth 25 minutes of your time. I'll show you exactly how we handle [their pain, their words]. I have [day] at [time] or [day] at [time] — which works? ... Great, invite is on its way before we hang up."

Send the calendar invite during the call. Confirm they accepted before ending. This single habit lifts demo show rates by double digits.

Branching LogicPage 5 of 7

When the answers go sideways.

What you hearWhat it meansWhat to do
"Just doing research." No trigger. Low urgency. Shorten the call. Offer a resource instead of a demo. Set a 60-day follow-up. Do not push a demo on a researcher.
"My boss told me to look into this." You're talking to a scout, not the buyer. Treat the scout as an ally: "What does your boss need to see to feel good about this?" Then ask to have the boss at the demo.
"We're comparing 3 or 4 tools." Active evaluation. Real deal, real competition. Ask: "What would make one of them the obvious winner?" Their criteria becomes your demo agenda.
"We tried a tool like yours and it didn't stick." Burned buyer. Skepticism, not resistance. Ask exactly why it failed. Address that specific failure in the demo before anything else.
"What does this cost?" (early) Either price-sensitive or efficiency-minded. Give the range immediately and confidently, then: "Is that in the realm of workable?" Hiding price loses velocity buyers.
Answers are vague on every question Not a real evaluation. Qualify out politely. "It sounds like the timing might not be right — totally fine. Want me to check back in a quarter?"

Scoring the CallPage 6 of 7

Qualify in or out, on paper.

After every discovery call, score the deal in under a minute. Five dimensions, one point each.

Dimension1 point if…
TriggerA specific event created this search (not "just looking").
FitCompany and persona match the ICP rubric.
CostBuyer articulated a real cost of the status quo.
PathYou know who signs and how the approval works.
TimelineBuyer named a date they want this solved by.
  • 4-5 points: Demo booked, deal in pipeline at full confidence.
  • 3 points: Demo booked, but flag the missing dimension and resolve it at the demo.
  • 0-2 points: Nurture, don't demo. Resource + scheduled follow-up.

Pre-call checklist (5 minutes before)

  • Looked at their website and noted one specific thing to reference.
  • Checked the lead source and any form answers they gave.
  • Confirmed their title matches a persona in the ICP rubric.
  • Calendar open to book the demo live.
  • CRM record open to score the call immediately after.

Post-call workflow (within 15 minutes)

  • Score the deal (5-point rubric above) and log it in CRM.
  • Write the pain in the buyer's exact words in the opportunity record.
  • Confirm the demo invite was accepted.
  • Send a 3-line recap email: what you heard, what the demo will cover, when it is.

Next StepsPage 7 of 7

Making this stick on your team.

A framework on paper changes nothing. The shift happens when every rep runs the same five questions, every call gets scored, and call reviews inspect against the structure week after week.

Three changes to make this stick

  • Put the five questions in the CRM. Five fields on the opportunity record. A deal can't advance to demo stage until they're filled.
  • Review two recorded discovery calls per rep per week. Inspect for the structure, not the style. Reps keep their own voice.
  • Track discovery-to-demo and demo-show rates. Both lift within 30 days when the framework is actually being run.

When to bring in outside help

If your reps each run their own version of discovery and the results are uneven across the team, the issue is almost never talent. It's that the framework lives in your best rep's head instead of in a document everyone trains on.
