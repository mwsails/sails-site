The Sales Playbook Starter Template.

The 9-section skeleton of a working B2B SaaS sales playbook. Each section comes with the prompts to answer, the order to build in, and the test that tells you it's done. Built for velocity motions.

How to Use ThisPage 2 of 8

A playbook is a working document, not a slide deck.

The test of a real playbook: a new rep could rebuild your sales motion from the document alone. If your reps don't reference it weekly, it's a museum exhibit, not a playbook.

The rules

  • Build in dependency order, not importance order. You can't write the demo framework before the ICP. The 9 sections below are sequenced so each builds on the last.
  • Pull from won deals, not theory. Look at your last 10 customers. What they have in common is your ICP. What your best rep says on calls is your messaging. Extract, don't invent.
  • Done-enough beats perfect. Each section has a "done when" test. Pass the test, move on. You'll revise quarterly anyway.
  • One owner, blocked time. Playbooks built "in spare time" don't get built. Assign an owner and block the weeks, or bring in outside help while the team keeps selling.

Sections 1-2Page 3 of 8
1ICP Definition + Qualification RubricWeek 1

Answer these prompts

  • Firmographics: company size, industry, geography, stage, tech stack of your last 10 won deals?
  • Persona: exact title, what they own, what they're measured on?
  • Trigger events: what was happening at the company when each of your last 10 customers bought?
  • Anti-ICP: who do you refuse to sell to, and why? (Churned customers are the data here.)
  • Rubric: write 5-7 yes/no questions a rep can answer about any lead in under 60 seconds.

Done when · Someone who's never sold your product reads it and describes back a specific person at a specific kind of company with a specific problem. Generic = not done.

2Messaging FrameworkWeeks 1-2

Answer these prompts

  • The 15-second pitch: what you do, for whom, solving what — in two sentences a rep can say cold.
  • Value proposition per persona: what does each role actually care about?
  • 3-5 proof points: specific customer outcomes with numbers.
  • Top 3 objections you hear, with a documented response for each.
  • "What we are not": the sentence that disqualifies wrong-fit buyers fast.

Done when · Three different reps deliver the 15-second pitch and sound substantively the same.

Sections 3-4Page 4 of 8
3Pipeline Stages + Exit CriteriaWeeks 2-3

Answer these prompts

  • Define 4-7 stages from first contact to closed-won. Name them after what happened, not what's next.
  • For each stage: what specifically must be true for a deal to exit it? Write it as checkable facts.
  • Time-in-stage benchmark: how long does a healthy deal sit in each stage?
  • Stalled-deal protocol: what happens when a deal exceeds the benchmark?

Done when · You can pull any random deal from the CRM and place it in the right stage from the criteria alone, without asking the rep.

4Discovery FrameworkWeeks 3-4

Answer these prompts

  • The 5-7 questions every rep asks, in order. (Start from the Velocity Discovery Framework if you have it.)
  • Branching logic: if the buyer says X, what's the follow-up?
  • The do-not-ask list: questions that waste time in a 20-minute call.
  • The next-step script: how every discovery call ends with a demo booked live.

Done when · Three recorded discovery calls from three reps show the same structural moves, even if the wording differs.

5Demo FrameworkWeeks 3-4

Answer these prompts

  • The opening: how the first 3 minutes recap discovery and set the agenda.
  • The standard flow: which 3-4 features, in what order, each tied to a stated pain.
  • Customization rules: what to add or skip based on discovery answers.
  • The pricing reveal: when and how price comes up (you bring it up, not the buyer).
  • The ask: how every demo ends with a clear yes/no/next-step, never "let me think about it."

Done when · Demos across reps land within 5 minutes of each other on length, and every one ends with an explicit ask.

6Outbound Sequence DesignWeek 5

Answer these prompts

  • Cadence: how many touches over how many days? (Velocity default: 12 touches, 14 days.)
  • Channel mix: email / phone / LinkedIn percentages for your ICP.
  • Theme per touch: cold opener, value follow-up, pattern interrupt, social proof, breakup.
  • Personalization rules: which touches require it, which run from the framework.
  • Stop conditions: when a prospect exits the sequence.

Done when · A new BDR can run a full sequence in their first week without inventing anything.

7Meeting Cadence FrameworkWeeks 5-6

Answer these prompts

  • Daily standup: 15 minutes, what three questions does it answer?
  • Weekly 1:1 per rep: how is time split between pipeline and coaching? (Hint: separate meetings.)
  • Weekly pipeline review: deal-by-deal with exit-criteria checks, not a forecast roll-up.
  • Monthly skill session: what gets taught, who teaches it?
  • Quarterly review: what does the team retro against?

Done when · Each meeting has a distinct purpose and none of them collapse into "what's happening with Acme."

8KPI FrameworkWeeks 5-6

Answer these prompts

  • Which 8-12 metrics predict outcomes in your motion? (Start: lead response time, demo conversion, cycle length by channel, pipeline coverage, activity-to-outcome.)
  • Target and threshold for each: what's healthy, what triggers action?
  • Data source and owner: where does each number come from, who updates it, how often?
  • Leading indicators at the top of the dashboard, lagging (revenue, win rate) below.

Done when · You can answer "is the team on track this month?" from the dashboard alone, without anecdotes.

9Hiring Profile + Org StructureWeeks 6-7

Answer these prompts

  • Rep profile per role: experience range, motion background, what to screen out.
  • Comp structure per role: base, variable, OTE, quota multiple, accelerators. (Use the Comp Plan Builder.)
  • Interview process: stages, what each tests, and a weighted scorecard.
  • Hiring sequence: who you hire when, and the trigger for each hire.
  • 90-day ramp plan: milestones at day 30, 60, 90.

Done when · You could hand the section to a recruiter and they could source candidates without a briefing call.

Weeks 7-8: Assembly

  • Consolidate all nine sections into one document with a table of contents.
  • Test with a fresh reader. Hand it to someone unfamiliar with your motion. Every place they hesitate is a gap. Fix the gaps.
  • Set the review cadence. Quarterly review date, named owner. A playbook that doesn't get updated decays into a museum exhibit within two quarters.

The most common failure mode

Building it in spare time. "After this quarter closes" becomes next quarter, becomes never. The teams that finish either block dedicated weeks for one owner, or bring in outside help to build it while the team keeps selling. Fitting it into the cracks is how playbooks stay 40% done forever.
